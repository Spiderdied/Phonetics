Branches of Phonetics

Core Definitions
Foundational terminology for the scientific study of language and speech sounds.

Linguistics

The scientific study of language.

Phonetics

The scientific study of speech sounds in all languages.

Phonology

The study of sound systems in a specific language.

Phoneme

The smallest unit of sound that can change meaning.

Consonant

A sound produced with some obstruction of the airflow.

Vowel

A sound produced with no obstruction of the airflow.

Place of Articulation

The place where the obstruction of airflow occurs.

Manner of Articulation

The way in which the airflow is obstructed.

Voicing

The vibration of the vocal cords during the production of sounds.

Types of Phonetics
Three branches that study different aspects of speech sounds.
🗣️

Articulatory

The study of how speech sounds are produced by the organs of speech (lips, tongue, teeth, etc.).

🌊

Acoustic

The study of the transmission of speech sounds through the air as sound waves.

👂

Auditory

The study of how speech sounds are received and perceived by the listener.

Manner of Articulation
How the airflow is shaped or obstructed during sound production.

Plosives (Stops)

Complete stop of airflow, then sudden release causing an explosion of air. E.g.: /p/, /b/, /t/, /d/, /k/, /g/

Fricatives

Partial narrowing of the vocal tract; air escapes gradually creating friction. E.g.: /f/, /v/, /s/, /z/, /ʃ/

Affricates

Complete stop followed by a gradual release, combining plosive and fricative qualities. E.g.: /tʃ/, /dʒ/

Nasals

Airflow is redirected through the nasal cavity. E.g.: /m/, /n/, /ŋ/

Lateral

Air passes along the sides of the tongue while the tip blocks the centre. E.g.: /l/

Glides (Approximants)

Produced with very little obstruction of airflow; similar in quality to a vowel. E.g.: /w/, /j/

Place of Articulation
Where in the vocal tract the obstruction of airflow occurs.
01

Bilabial

Both lips come together. E.g.: /p/, /b/, /m/, /w/

02

Labiodental

Upper teeth touch the lower lip. E.g.: /f/, /v/

03

Dental

Tongue tip placed against or between the upper teeth. E.g.: /θ/, /ð/

04

Alveolar

Tongue tip touches the alveolar ridge (just behind upper teeth). E.g.: /t/, /d/, /s/, /z/, /n/, /l/

05

Post-Alveolar

Tongue blade raised behind the alveolar ridge. E.g.: /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /tʃ/, /dʒ/, /r/

06

Palatal

Tongue body raised toward the hard palate. E.g.: /j/

07

Velar

Back of tongue raised toward the soft palate (velum). E.g.: /k/, /g/, /ŋ/

08

Glottal

Constriction at the level of the glottis (vocal cords). E.g.: /h/, /ʔ/

Phonetic Transcription
Phonetic transcription uses the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) to represent sounds precisely, regardless of spelling.

Phonetic transcription is written between /slashes/ for broad (phonemic) transcription, or [brackets] for narrow (phonetic) transcription. Spelling and pronunciation often differ in English — transcription reveals the true sound structure of words.
