Welcome to Phonetics!

Phonetics is the scientific study of speech sounds. It helps us understand how sounds are physically produced by our mouth, vocal cords, and breath.

في القسم ده، هنبسط كل حاجة عشان تفهم الإنجليزي صح وتتكلم بثقة.

Why learn Phonetics?

  • Better Pronunciation: You will learn exactly how to move your mouth to sound natural.
  • Clearer Listening: Understanding sounds helps you catch fast speech easily.
  • Reading Transcriptions: You can read any dictionary to know how a word is pronounced without hearing it!

The Organs of Speech

To produce English sounds, we use different parts of our vocal tract. The main ones include:

  • Lungs: Provide the airflow.
  • Vocal Cords (Larynx): Vibrate to make voiced sounds.
  • Tongue & Lips: Shape the air to create different vowels and consonants.
  • Alveolar Ridge: The bumpy part just behind your upper teeth.

English Consonants

Consonants are sounds produced by blocking the air as it leaves your mouth. We classify them using three main features:

  • Place of Articulation: WHERE the sound is made (e.g., Lips, Teeth).
  • Manner of Articulation: HOW the air is blocked (e.g., Stopped completely, or pushed through a narrow gap).
  • Voicing: Are the vocal cords vibrating (Voiced) or not (Voiceless)?
/p/

Place: Bilabial (Both lips)

Manner: Stop (Air is blocked, then released)

Voicing: Voiceless

Examples: pen, apple, stop

/b/

Place: Bilabial (Both lips)

Manner: Stop

Voicing: Voiced

Examples: boy, rabbit, web

/f/

Place: Labiodental (Bottom lip & top teeth)

Manner: Fricative (Air pushes through a gap)

Voicing: Voiceless

Examples: fish, phone, laugh

/v/

Place: Labiodental

Manner: Fricative

Voicing: Voiced

Examples: van, over, have

/θ/

Place: Dental (Tongue between teeth)

Manner: Fricative

Voicing: Voiceless

Examples: think, math, three

/m/

Place: Bilabial

Manner: Nasal (Air flows through the nose)

Voicing: Voiced

Examples: man, summer, comb

English Vowels

Vowels are produced with an open mouth—air flows freely without being blocked. They are categorized by tongue height, tongue position (front/back), and lip rounding.

1. Short Vowels

/ɪ/

Description: High front, unrounded, short

Examples: sit, hit, myth

/æ/

Description: Low front, unrounded, short

Examples: cat, bad, map

/ə/

Description: Schwa (mid-central, relaxed)

Examples: about, sofa

2. Long Vowels

/i:/

Description: High front, unrounded, long

Examples: see, beat, key

/u:/

Description: High back, rounded, long

Examples: food, blue, two

3. Diphthongs

A diphthong is a combination of two vowel sounds within the same syllable. Your tongue glides from one position to another.

/aɪ/

Examples: my, like, fly

/eɪ/

Examples: say, make, eight

Phonetic Transcription (IPA)

The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) is a system where each sound is represented by exactly one symbol. This helps you pronounce words perfectly, even if English spelling is confusing!

دلوقتي هنشوف إزاي نكتب الكلمة زي ما بننطقها بالظبط.

Word Transcription Explanation
cat /kæt/ 3 sounds: /k/ + /æ/ + /t/
ship /ʃɪp/ 'sh' makes one sound /ʃ/, not two.
church /tʃɜ:tʃ/ 'ch' is the affricate /tʃ/.
phone /fəʊn/ 'ph' is pronounced as /f/. The 'e' is silent.
think /θɪŋk/ 'th' is /θ/ and 'nk' is /ŋ/ + /k/.
