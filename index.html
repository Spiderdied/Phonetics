Core Definitions

  • Phonetics: The branch of linguistics that studies the actual physical sounds of speech. [span_2](start_span)It looks at how sounds are produced, transmitted, and received[span_2](end_span).
    • [span_3](start_span)
  • Phonology: Studies how sounds function in a specific language and looks at sound patterns and rules[span_3](end_span).
    • [span_4](start_span)
  • Phoneme: The distinct units of sound that can differentiate words[span_4](end_span). [span_5](start_span)English has 44 phonemes in total[span_5](end_span).
    • [span_6](start_span)
  • Organs of Speech: The body parts that work together to produce language sounds, shaping airflow from the lungs [cite: 35-36]. [cite_start]Key organs include the Lungs, Larynx (Voice Box), Pharynx, Tongue, Lips, Teeth, Alveolar Ridge, Hard Palate, and Soft Palate [cite: 38-62].

English Consonants

[cite_start]

English has 24 consonant sounds[span_6](end_span). [span_7](start_span)They are produced with some degree of obstruction in the vocal tract[span_7](end_span).

[span_8](start_span)

Key Features[span_8](end_span)

  • Place of Articulation: Where the sound is made (e.g., lips, teeth, roof of mouth).
  • Manner of Articulation: How the airflow is controlled (e.g., stopped, narrowed for friction).
    • [span_9](start_span)
  • Voicing: Voiced sounds vibrate the vocal cords; voiceless sounds do not [cite: 43-44].

English Vowels

[cite_start]

English has 20 vowel sounds[span_9](end_span). [span_10](start_span)[span_11](start_span)Unlike consonants, vowels are produced with an open vocal tract and no significant obstruction of airflow[span_10](end_span)[span_11](end_span).

    [span_12](start_span)

Steps to Transcribe [cite: 154-155]

  1. Listen to the word and identify syllable boundaries.
  2. Break down into sounds (e.g., "potato" -> /pəˈteɪtəʊ/).
  3. Identify consonants and vowels.
  4. Mark stress: primary stress uses ' (e.g., /ˈbʌtə/).
